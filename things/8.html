FROM THE ARCHIVES

Before there was tar, there was pitch! Tasty and black: a tasty snack: this is an accurate description of both. In these pieces from the ybot archives (modified, to preserve privacy, from instant-message originals) Mr Maestro eats his fill.

Perfect Pitch

"How's that for perfect pitch, eh?"

Mr Maestro is in no fit condition to answer; his mouth is gummed up with the irresistible black polymer. The pitch is as molasses in terms of deliciosity and viscosity—except much more delici- and viscous than that sugar product. Tasty tar; beautiful bitumen; amazing asphalt. Another distinction, MM begins to realise, between this palatial pitch and treacle both light and dark is that one tends to tire of the taste of those sweeteners: but there is nothing sickly sweet about this rich and rewarding resin. MM continues to pile gobs of gobs into his gob; the spoon becomes blacker and blacker, as does his mouth. No toothbrush could now restore MM's erstwhile dentition; the actual structural integrity of his teeth and his gums and the relationship therebetween is quite seriously in question. MM loves the pitch; MM craves the pitch, even when the pitch is in his mouth; MM loves the pitch. He loves it so much that he now dispenses with the dispenser—lobs the spoon away—and plunges his face into the drum of pitch. He breathes in the delicious pitch. The pitch seems paltry in the face of MM's overwhelming desire; he feels curiously unsatisfied, even as his head is wholly surrounded by wonderful, wonderful pitch.

"How's that for perfect pitch, eh?"

Fever Pitch

"Pitch delicious
Pitch divine
Drink the pitch as if it's wine!

Pitch aplenty
Pitch galore
Drink the pitch, then drink some more!"

The chorus have reached fever pitch. And Mr Maestro is in terrific form; he conducts them to victory.


Thus concludes our archival dive.
